Part I · Own Your Process 
Since late 2025, coding agents have taken a massive leap in capability — and a wave of heavyweight 
frameworks (GSD, BMAD, Spec-Kit and the like) has rushed in to “own” the process for you. The 
premise of this guide is the opposite: you, the engineer, should own your process. What follows is a 
process you can run today, then iterate on and make your own. 
The approach rests on a single conviction: software-engineering fundamentals matter more in the AI 
age, not less. The heuristics built up over thirty-plus years of industry experience — domain-driven 
design, tracer bullets, test-driven development, deep modules, designing the system every day — are 
not legacy baggage to be automated away. They are the rails that make an agent reliable. Throughout 
this guide we will repeatedly wave actual books at you, because the classics already contain the 
answers; they simply apply harder to a stateless, forgetful agent than they ever did to a human. 
The “code is cheap” thesis is wrong 
A popular argument says that because AI churns out code so cheaply, code quality matters less — you 
can simply regenerate your way out of a bad codebase. This is backwards. Quality matters more now, 
and the agent is more sensitive to it than a human, for three reasons: 
• It is stateless. A human survives a bad codebase by accumulating memory of where things live 
and why. A cleared agent has none of that — it is “a new developer joining your team every 
twenty minutes with zero institutional knowledge.” A garbage codebase gives it no chance. 
• It copies your codebase by default. Of the three inputs an agent has — your prompt, your 
steering files, and the codebase itself — the codebase is the strongest signal. The agent imitates 
the patterns it already sees, even when your steering explicitly tells it not to. 
• It cannot make the entropy-reducing commits. Agents are excellent at adding features and 
fixing bugs, and poor at the rarer, harder redesign commits that pay down complexity. So they are 
a software-entropy accelerator: more commits, faster, most of them nudging the “kludge meter” 
upward. 
 
The principle. Garbage in, garbage out. Cheap code just accelerates you toward a maxed-
out kludge meter — a codebase so complex that even the AI can no longer change it. 
Investing in quality is now a force multiplier, not a tax. 
How to read this guide 
Part II explains how the agent actually works — the constraints that every later decision is designed 
around. Part III reframes those constraints as four failure modes and ties each to the engineering 
canon. Part IV walks the seven-phase workflow end to end, and closes with the wayfinder map — the 
second front door for work too big for one session. Part V is a reference to the skill library; Part VI is the 
practical setup and run-book, including how the workflow scales to a team. Part VII is the AFK setup 
and Sandcastle process. Appendices give a vocabulary, a reading list, and a command reference. 
Read Parts II–III once for the mental model; thereafter Parts IV–VII and the appendices are the working 
reference.

Part II · How the Agent Actually Works 
“Large language models aren’t junior developers. They’re something fundamentally weirder and 
more constrained than that.” 
If you don’t understand the constraints, “you’re going to end up blaming AI when in fact you’re just 
working against how the models are supposed to work.” Four interlocking constraints drive the entire 
workflow. Fight them and you get slop; design around them and the agent becomes genuinely powerful. 
Constraint 1 — Attention degrades: the efficient zone and the inefficient zone 
A model does not merely store tokens; it stores every token’s relationship to every other token. Add one 
token and the number of relationships grows quadratically — “like adding a team to a football league, 
where the number of games played massively increases as you add teams.” Four tokens make six 
relationships; eight make twenty-eight; a hundred make roughly five thousand. Crucially, each token’s 
attention budget is finite and does not grow as the context grows. So as a session fills, the signal on the 
relationships that matter gets spread thinner and thinner. 
Early in a session the agent is in the efficient zone: sharp, focused, attends to everything, reasons 
cleanly. As context fills it drifts into the inefficient zone: sloppier, more forgetful, prone to hallucination 
— and, tellingly, it begins to struggle to recall information sitting right there in its own context window. 
As a working heuristic, the efficient zone is roughly the first 100,000 tokens or so — on frontier models 
the inefficient zone commonly begins around 125K–150K, though the exact line is debated, and the 
slide is a gentle curve rather than a wall. (In the 200k-window era this was “get paranoid around the 
40% mark”; some argue 60–70%; everyone agrees the effect is real.) Matt’s own working ceiling 
corroborates the numbers: keep a day-task session under ~150k. (Matt’s public materials and the AI 
Coding Dictionary call these the “smart zone” and “dumb zone,” a coinage credited to Dex Horthy.) A 
useful continue-or-clear heuristic: if a dumber model could finish the task from here, keep going; if not, 
clear. 
Gotcha. Million-token windows (e.g. Opus / Sonnet models) do not move the efficient zone. 
“All that’s changed is they’ve shipped a lot more inefficient zone.” Think in raw token counts, 
not percentages, and stay paranoid about context usage. Fable notably has a 200K context, 
nearly pure efficient zone. 
Constraint 2 — Hallucination 
Hallucination is not a bug; it is what happens when you ask the model about something outside its 
fuzzy, compressed knowledge. “The model wants to help so badly that it fills the gaps with plausible-
sounding nonsense” — inventing methods, getting an API’s shape subtly wrong. It comes in two 
flavours: factuality (inventing facts) and faithfulness (drifting from the context it was given). 
Constraint 3 — You cannot use an LLM as a database 
Picture the model as having “compressed all of human knowledge down to a blurry JPEG — scribbled 
on the back of a napkin somewhere, and it can only half-remember it.” Recall from training data 
(parametric knowledge) is, by design, unreliable. But anything in the context window (contextual 
knowledge) is seen at full fidelity and answered reliably.

The principle. Put the documentation and code examples the task needs directly into 
context — don’t rely on the model’s memory. Anything newer than the training cutoff is a 
fabrication trap unless you load its docs. 
Constraint 4 — Statelessness 
“LLMs behave kind of like that guy from Memento, where every time he woke up he’d completely forget 
his entire life.” Every cleared context is a total reset. This is the constraint with the largest downstream 
consequence: because the agent has no memory, your codebase quality, documentation, and 
organization become the only persistent institutional knowledge. The real question becomes: is your 
repository friendly to a brand-new starter who arrives every twenty minutes? 
A fifth thing to expect — non-determinism 
Agents are next-token-chooser machines selecting by probability, so the same prompt against the 
same codebase yields different answers. Most responses cluster around something sensible; a few are 
weird outliers. You can make agents more consistent with feedback loops and tight scopes, but you 
cannot make them deterministic. “Ride the wave.” 
The harness, sessions, and turns 
The model itself only does next-token prediction. The harness is everything wrapped around it that 
turns it into an agent: tools, the system prompt, context-window management, permissions, and hooks. 
A session is one bounded run that starts empty, accumulates context across turns, and ends when it is 
cleared, closed, or compacted. A turn is one user message plus everything the agent does before 
yielding back to you. The workflow is harness-agnostic — any CLI-based agent works — but the 
examples use GitHub Copilot CLI (generally available since February 2026), typically run inside the VS 
Code integrated terminal. Copilot CLI’s harness adds slash commands, custom agents, and three 
interactive modes you cycle with Shift+Tab — standard → plan → autopilot (also settable as --
mode=interactive|plan|autopilot, or --plan to start planning) — all of which the workflow below leans 
on. Model choice is harness-level too: --model=MODEL (where auto lets Copilot select) or the 
COPILOT_MODEL environment variable, across the Claude, GPT, and Gemini families — the multi-model 
spread that Phase 7’s review stack exploits. Model lists churn fast; pick per task, don’t memorize 
versions. 
A useful way to hold the pieces is four concentric rings. The model sits in the middle — the part 
everyone talks about swapping and the part you control least. Around it sits the harness as defined 
above. Model plus harness is the agent — the thing that actually does the work. And wrapping the 
agent is the environment it operates inside: the file system, the codebase, the steering files, the 
feedback loops, the issue tracker. Chasing model upgrades misses where the leverage is: improving 
the harness and the environment pays roughly as much as a better model ever will — call it 50/50. You 
own the environment outright and you choose the harness; the model is the only ring you rent. 
Everything in this guide — steering files, skills, tickets, feedback loops — is environment and harness 
engineering.

Subagents — delegation that protects context 
The agent you talk to is the orchestrator (the lead developer). When it needs focused work — usually 
exploration — it spawns a subagent with a fresh context window. The subagent burns its own tokens in 
its own efficient zone, then reports a single summary back, so the orchestrator gets the findings without 
carrying every file read and tool call. 
• Subagents can run in parallel, with different prompts and cheaper/faster models — a smaller 
model on an explore phase is often “fast and really high quality.” 
• A subagent runs in its own session and returns one tool result. Subagents can spawn subagents 
of their own — nesting runs several levels deep — which unlocks model tiering: a big-model 
orchestrator delegating to mid-size subagents that spawn small explorers, each level roughly an 
order of magnitude cheaper.

• Word choice triggers depth. “Tell me the tech stack” reads a handful of files; “explore how X 
works” spawns a subagent that may spend 60k+ tokens and dozens of tool calls and return a 
summary “almost as good as if we’d written it ourselves.” 
Copilot CLI exposes this delegation as first-class machinery. A custom agent — 
.github/agents/NAME.agent.md in the repo, ~/.copilot/agents/ for personal ones, or org-wide via your 
organization’s .github repository — runs as a subagent with its own context; invoke one with /agent or 
pin it for a whole session with --agent=AGENT. /fleet fans an objective across several subagents 
working in parallel — the same exploration trade-off, resolved by the harness itself. And /subagents 
configures per-agent subagent model routing once, instead of specifying it in every prompt — so the 
model-tiering pattern (a Claude-family orchestrator routing slices to smaller GPT- or Gemini-family 
workers) becomes standing configuration. 
Why it works. There is an exploration trade-off: more tokens on exploring means fewer for 
implementing, but too little exploring means worse implementation. Subagents resolve it by 
doing the exploring elsewhere and returning only the conclusion. Because the agent is 
stateless, exploration is the foundation of every interaction — not a one-time setup. 
Context hygiene — clear, don’t compact 
When a session fills, you have three moves, and the order of preference matters: 
• Clearing ends the session and starts fresh with an empty context. This is the preferred default: 
more smart-zone time, fewer tokens spent summarizing, more predictable behavior, and cleaner 
separation between tasks. Good workflows are built to start repeatedly from clean context. 
• Compaction summarizes the conversation so it can continue (autocompact fires automatically 
near the limit). It preserves what was implemented, files touched, the ask, pending tasks, and any 
instruction you give just before it runs (e.g. “next goal is QA”). Reach for it only when you are still 
actively in the conversation — say, you just finished a big build and want focused QA without re-
exploring. 
• Handoff writes the session to a file so a fresh agent can continue (covered in Part IV). The rule of 
thumb: handoff forks to a fresh session; compaction continues the current one. 
Gotcha. Every compaction leaves “sediment.” Repeated compaction layers summaries on 
summaries and makes output steadily less predictable — autocompact is the worst offender, 
pulling in context just to shrink it again. Use compaction to rescue hard-won context for one 
tough follow-up; never build your workflow on it. 
Steering — keeping a stateless agent on your rails 
Steering is how you keep a forgetful agent aligned with your team’s standards. The naive approach — 
one giant AGENTS.md — collects sediment of its own: dozens of global instructions, most irrelevant to 
any given request, all loaded on every request and all competing with the actual prompt. 
Keep the standing brief ruthlessly small 
AGENTS.md (the open standard Copilot CLI reads natively) sits right below the system prompt and 
loads on every single request. Respect the instruction budget: frontier thinking models follow only 
~150–200 instructions with reasonable consistency (smaller and non-thinking models, fewer). So the 
ruthless minimum is: a one-sentence project description (it acts like a role prompt — “This is a React 
component library for accessible data visualization”), the package manager if it isn’t the default, and 
any non-standard build/typecheck commands. “That’s honestly it.”

• Never auto-generate it. Init scripts flood the file with “useful for most scenarios” cruft, prioritizing 
comprehensiveness over restraint. 
• Never document file paths. They are the worst kind of staleness: “authentication lives in 
src/auth/handlers.ts” becomes a lie the moment someone renames the file, and the agent reads 
that lie on every request. Describe capabilities and shape, not structure; let the agent generate 
just-in-time docs during planning. 
Progressive disclosure 
Borrowed from UI/UX: a bad interface dumps all hundred options into one flat list; a good one reduces 
the upfront choices and lets you navigate to the rest. Applied to steering, move grouped rules out of the 
standing brief into separate linked files, leaving only context pointers (“For TypeScript conventions, see 
docs/typescript.md”). The React Router rules load only when the agent is writing React Router code. 
Files can nest and can link out to external docs; agents navigate doc hierarchies efficiently. 
Agent skills — the built-in form of progressive disclosure 
A skill is a folder (a SKILL.md plus optional scripts, docs, and images) that the agent can discover. By 
default the agent sees only the skill’s name and description; it loads the full instructions only when it 
actually calls the skill. Skills can themselves link to deeper files, so a lean SKILL.md can sit in front of 
an entire framework’s documentation. Copilot CLI reads project skills from .github/skills/, 
.claude/skills/, or .agents/skills/ (project-local, shareable via git), and personal skills from 
~/.copilot/skills/ or ~/.agents/skills/; manage them in-CLI with /skills add | remove.

Writing great skills — predictability is the root virtue 
A skill exists to wrangle determinism out of a stochastic system. The root virtue is predictability — the 
agent taking the same process every run, not producing the same output — and every authoring 
decision below serves it. 
The invocation choice is an economics question. A model-invoked skill keeps a description the agent 
sees on every request: it buys autonomy with context load, and every context pointer carries 
unpredictability — “the model may just choose not to follow it.” A user-invoked skill costs nothing in 
context but spends cognitive load: you are the index that must remember it exists. Prefer user-invoked 
for orchestration; when user-invoked skills multiply past what you can remember, the cure is a router 
skill — which is exactly what /ask-matt is. 
Structure: steps and reference. A skill’s content decomposes into steps (ordered actions, each ending 
on a checkable completion criterion) and reference (rules and vocabulary). Reference every run needs 
stays inline in SKILL.md; reference only some branches reach moves behind a context pointer into a

Steer with leading words. A short, loaded phrase — “tracer bullet,” “vertical slice” — packs a dense 
concept the model already knows into two words. The agent repeats leading words back in its 
reasoning, which doubles as your verification that the skill is actually steering. 
Hide the future; prune the no-ops. Agents under-invest in a step when they can see the goal past it — 
the real diagnosis of plan mode’s weakness, and the reason the grill and the spec are separate skills: 
the agent only sees one step at a time. And prune relentlessly with the deletion test: if removing a 
sentence wouldn’t change the agent’s behavior, it’s a no-op — kill it. /writing-great-skills holds the full

Automatic memory 
Copilot CLI’s experimental /memory layers your steering the same way: user memory (your personal 
brief), project memory (the checked-in AGENTS.md), and auto-generated memory the agent writes to 
steer itself over time, injected alongside AGENTS.md. It is powerful but new, and the worst case is that 
it drifts out of date with the repo. 
Gotcha. Memory and steering files go stale, and stale markdown actively poisons context 
because the agent trusts it on every request. Audit your auto-generated memory, 
research.md, ADRs, and your glossary every couple of weeks and prune anything that no 
longer matches the repo. Or better yet, disable it. 
AX — agent experience 
Underneath every later technique is one idea: agent experience (AX) — how well the environment is 
set up for an agent to do good work. Where DX (developer experience) optimizes for humans, AX 
optimizes for a stateless collaborator: deterministic checks it can run, an architecture it can navigate, a 
domain language it can reuse, and free context it isn’t forced to waste. Organizing a codebase for AX 
is, in Matt’s words, “probably one of the highest-impact things you can do.”

Part III · Four Failure Modes and the Engineering Canon 
Everything in the workflow exists to defeat one of four recurring failure modes. Each failure has a fix, 
and each fix is lifted straight from a well-known engineering text. This is the heart of the “real engineers” 
framing: the classics already solved these problems for human teams; agents just make the lessons 
non-negotiable. 
Failure 1 — “The agent didn’t do what I want” 
“No-one knows exactly what they want.” — David Thomas & Andrew Hunt, The Pragmatic 
Programmer 
The oldest failure in software is misalignment: you assumed the developer understood you, then saw 
what they built. With an agent the communication gap is identical. Plan mode does not fix it — it 
truncates the interview, asking one or two questions before dumping a wall of text that’s easy to skim 
and approve while still misaligned. Frederick Brooks named the missing thing in The Design of Design: 
“A design concept isn’t an asset or a plan. It’s the ephemeral understanding shared between all 
participants.” — Frederick P. Brooks, The Design of Design 
The fix is grilling: the agent interviews you Socratically, one decision at a time, walking down every 
branch of the decision tree before any code is written, and recommending an answer for each question. 
Skills: /grill-me (general) and /grill-with-docs (against your domain model). 
Failure 2 — “The agent is way too verbose” 
“With a ubiquitous language, conversations among developers and expressions of the code are all 
derived from the same domain model.” — Eric Evans, Domain-Driven Design 
Agents are usually dropped into a project and left to infer the jargon, so they use twenty words where 
one will do. The fix is a shared, ubiquitous language: a CONTEXT.md glossary at the repo root that 
defines the domain’s terms. Compare “there’s a problem when a lesson inside a section of a course is 
given a spot in the file system” with “there’s a problem with the materialization cascade.” The concision 
compounds session after session, and the benefits run deeper than brevity: variables, functions, and 
files get named consistently; the codebase becomes easier to navigate; and the agent spends fewer 
tokens thinking because it has a tighter language to think in. Skills: /grill-with-docs and /domain-
modeling; hard, surprising, irreversible decisions are captured as ADRs. 
Failure 3 — “The code doesn’t work” 
“Always take small, deliberate steps. The rate of feedback is your speed limit. Never take on a task 
that’s too big.” — David Thomas & Andrew Hunt, The Pragmatic Programmer 
Even when you are aligned on what to build, the agent can still produce code that doesn’t run — 
because it is flying blind, with no signal on how its code actually behaves. The fix is the usual tranche of 
feedback loops: static types, browser access, and automated tests, with a red-green loop at the center. 
Writing a failing test first — and running it to watch it fail — gives the agent a concrete target it can 
iterate against, and is “the single most consistent way to improve agent output.” Skills: /tdd and 
/diagnosing-bugs; pre-commit hooks make the loops deterministic.

Failure 4 — “We built a ball of mud” 
“Invest in the design of the system every day.” — Kent Beck, Extreme Programming Explained 
“The best modules are deep. They allow a lot of functionality to be accessed through a simple 
interface.” — John Ousterhout, A Philosophy of Software Design 
Because agents radically speed up coding, they also accelerate software entropy — codebases get 
more complex at an unprecedented rate. To an agent, a fragmented codebase of dozens of tiny 
modules with scattered exports is near-unreadable: it must re-trace the import/export graph from 
scratch every session and can’t see at a glance where functionality lives, and the fragmentation forces 
brittle, tightly-coupled tests that break on every change, destroying the very feedback loops the agent 
depends on. The fix is to care about design every day and to build deep modules: a thin, stable 
interface hiding a large implementation. Skills: /codebase-design (the vocabulary) and /improve-
codebase-architecture (the every-few-days upkeep). More on deep modules in Part IV, Phase 7.

Part IV · The Seven-Phase Workflow 
The whole system converges on one through-line: Grill → Research → Prototype → Spec → Tickets → 
Implement → Review. Phases 1–3 iterate to harden an idea before you commit to a spec; Phases 4–5 
turn it into a spec and a backlog of tickets; Phase 6 ships; Phase 7 reviews and feeds lessons back into 
the next Grill. The steering layer from Part II wraps everything. Phase 1 has two entries — grill the idea 
that fits one session, or wayfind the one that doesn’t: /wayfinder runs Phases 1–3 as a map of decision 
tickets, and both entries merge at the spec. Research and Prototype are optional; the rest are the 
spine. “AI coding gives you permission to build bigger” — and this is how you exercise that permission 
without drowning. 
 
The seven-phase workflow, the four failure modes, and the constraints they answer. 
Phase 1 — Grill: harden the idea 
Goal: reach a precise, shared understanding before any code — the GRILL step of the grill / execute / 
clear loop. Run /grill-with-docs for code work (it also builds your domain model) or /grill-me for non-code 
planning — or /wayfinder when the idea is bigger than one session (see the end of this Part): it has 
become Matt’s own default entry for anything non-trivial, and it degrades to a plain grilling session when 
it senses no map is needed. In Copilot CLI you enter plan mode with Shift+Tab before describing the 
work, then run the grilling skill so the agent interrogates you one question at a time. 
Don’t let the agent jump to implementation, and don’t trust plan mode’s habit of dumping a plan before 
you actually understand each other. Instead, run a grilling conversation. The skill itself is famously short

— essentially: “Interview me relentlessly about every aspect of this until we reach a shared 
understanding. Walk down each branch of the decision tree, resolving dependencies between 
decisions one by one. For each question, provide your recommended answer. Ask one at a time, and if 
a question can be answered by exploring the codebase, explore instead.” The grilling primitive was 
reworded in July 2026 to generalize beyond code: it now grills any plan, decision, or idea. 
• One question at a time keeps your focus moving decision to decision; a single big plan document 
invites you to get lazy and skim. 
• A recommended answer per question lets you move fast — the most common reply is just 
“correct.” 
• It’s a dialogue, not an interrogation. Drive it. The highest-value move is to ask “what’s reversible 
— what would be hard to roll back from?” which often flips a recommendation toward the more 
reversible option. 
• Let it explore to answer its own questions rather than pre-answering everything. 
• Facts are looked up; decisions are yours. If a fact can be found by exploring the codebase, the 
agent should look it up rather than ask; every decision, though, gets put to you explicitly. And say 
“I don’t care” when you truly don’t — it delegates that decision cleanly and keeps the grill moving. 
The facts/decisions split is also what stops a session from “self-grilling” — exploring the codebase 
and answering its own questions — and the session ends behind a confirmation gate: nothing 
gets enacted until you confirm shared understanding. 
Why it works. The decision tree comes from Brooks’s The Design of Design (his name for 
it: the design tree): walk every branch before committing to code. Real sessions run roughly 
16 questions for a small feature and 30–50 for a complex one. A fully-grilled context window 
is “pure gold” — if budget remains, implement in that same window; if not, write a spec as 
the handoff artifact. Don’t clear a grilled context just to write a spec. 
Phase 2 — Research: cache the expensive explores 
Goal: resolve unknowns once so later runs don’t repay for them. Run /research to delegate it: a 
background agent investigates the question against primary sources — official docs, source code, 
specs, never a secondary write-up of them — and leaves a cited markdown file in the repo while you 
keep working. Research feeds the thinking; it doesn’t replace it: the file is something to take into the 
grill. In Copilot CLI the skill runs naturally as a custom agent, and several open questions can burn 
down in parallel via /fleet; note also the CLI’s new experimental native /research command, which does 
the same job harness-side. 
Some work has long explore phases (integrating an external service whose docs aren’t local). Inside an 
AFK loop spanning many context windows, every run repeats that long explore; over ten runs it gets 
very expensive. Before the loop, gather what the build needs — external docs, an approach decision 
(SSE vs WebSockets) — and cache it into a local asset, usually research.md. Every later run reads the 
file instead of re-exploring, keeping runs in the efficient zone with less token spend. 
The principle. Treat research as a human-in-the-loop task — your taste decides which 
library or approach goes into research.md, and that judgment shapes everything 
downstream. Give the file a tightly-managed lifecycle; stale research hurts as much as any 
stale steering file.

Phase 3 — Prototype: make it concrete, apply taste 
Goal: when conversation is too low-fidelity, build a quick, rough artifact to react to — and impose your 
taste before autonomous implementation. Run /prototype, which routes to one of two branches. 
• UI variations — several radically different designs on one throwaway route, toggled from a 
floating bar, so you can walk the decision tree by eye (“I like A but take a bit from C”). 
• Logic/state — a tiny interactive terminal app that pushes a complex or over-time data model 
through its hard-to-reason cases. 
Prototypes work best integrated into the real code they are prototyping for — next to the module or 
page in question, clearly named as a prototype — because reacting to the real thing beats reacting to a 
facsimile. Prototype is throwaway by design: one command to run, no persistence, skip the polish — 
but when it’s done, capture, don’t dispose: fold the validated decision into the real code, then commit 
the prototype itself to a throwaway branch off main as a primary source, with a context pointer from the 
implementation issue and the verdict recorded alongside the question it settled. The main branch keeps 
only the validated decision. It pairs naturally with research — prototype each researched option, QA 
them, feed the winner into the spec. 
For UI work, pair the prototype with a design skill — the community front-end-design skill from skills.sh, 
or your own design language layered on top — and ask for many variations in a single HTML file (Burke 
Holland generates twenty at once, driving a Claude-family model in the demo) so you can compare 
them side by side. Preview them in VS Code’s integrated browser with the Live Preview extension, then 
let the one you pick feed straight into the prompt — and, per the doctrine above, park the mock file on a 
throwaway branch rather than deleting it: it is a primary source for the implementation ticket. 
Gotcha. Prototyping is not for bug fixing (you already know the desired behavior) or for 
extending existing features (don’t prototype another modal when you have a hundred). 
Reach for it when you’re redesigning a whole system or facing a genuine “how should this 
feel?” question that grilling can’t resolve. 
Phase 4 — Spec: write the destination 
Goal: synthesize everything learned into a reviewable spec that scales a build beyond a single context 
window. Run /to-spec, which turns the current conversation into a spec — the document you may know 
as a PRD; “spec” is now the single through-line term, since what it holds is broader than product 
requirements (the skill was /to-prd in earlier editions). It does not re-interview you. 
The spec has a Problem statement, a Solution, an extensive list of User Stories (desired behavior in 
plain language, from Agile), Implementation Decisions, Testing Decisions, and an explicit Out of Scope. 
The user stories are the most important part — they pin down behavior. This version also sketches the 
major modules, hunts for deep modules (a small interface hiding a large implementation), and asks 
which modules you want tested and at what boundary. It sketches the seams at which the feature will 
be tested — preferring existing seams over new ones, placed as high as possible: the fewer seams 
across the codebase the better, and the ideal number is one. It checks those seams with you, then 
publishes straight to ready-for-agent — tickets born from a spec don’t need triage. 
• Respect ADRs and use the domain glossary — the spec should read in your project’s language. 
• No file paths or code snippets in a spec — they go stale fast (the one exception is a decision-
encoding snippet lifted from a prototype). 
• Commit the spec and skim the user stories for clarity to an outsider before slicing it up. The spec 
is a summarization artifact — if the grill did its job, alignment already happened, and you rarely

Why it works. A big task bursts into the inefficient zone or doesn’t fit at all. The decades-old 
answer is to break it down so all the work happens in the efficient zone — and that needs a 
written destination. The spec is where you impose taste and where the build scales beyond 
one window. 
Phase 5 — Tickets: don’t plan, Kanban 
Goal: turn the destination into a journey the agent can grab piece by piece — a dependency graph, not 
a rigid multi-phase plan. Run /to-tickets to slice, and /triage to keep the backlog clean. One bridge for 
this guide: a ticket is a GitHub Issue, and the wayfinder’s decision tickets (end of this Part) are sub-
issues of a parent map issue. Triage also covers external PRs — a PR is an issue with attached code: 
same roles, same states, same machine. 
Humans don’t rigidly plan everything in one document; once we know the destination we make a 
Kanban board of issues with blocking relationships. A board is more flexible (add an issue by 
describing a blocking relationship — no plan rewrite), parallelizable (independent issues run at once), 
and infinitely extensible (QA feedback becomes new issues that grow the graph organically). 
Tracer bullets — the unit of a ticket 
An agent’s sycophancy makes it want to deliver a complete solution in one leap, building horizontal 
layers in isolation — all endpoints, then all auth, then all logging — before discovering the database 
connection string was wrong. The Pragmatic Programmer calls this outrunning your headlights. A tracer 
bullet is the opposite: a small end-to-end vertical slice that touches every layer at once (schema → API 
→ UI → tests). Build a tiny slice, test it immediately, get feedback, then expand. 
“Two well-chosen words can completely change the way AI prioritises and plans. The agent already 
knows the concept — just say ‘use tracer bullets.’” 
/to-tickets locates the spec (or works from the conversation), explores the codebase, drafts vertical-
slice tickets — each declaring its blocking edges — and marks each HITL (needs human judgment) or 
AFK (can be merged unattended, preferred), always adds a final HITL QA issue blocked by all the 
others, then quizzes you on granularity, dependencies, and the HITL/AFK split before publishing in 
dependency order. The same artifact works on two tracker shapes: on a local tracker the edges are text 
in an ordered tickets.md you work top-to-bottom by hand; on a real tracker — GitHub Issues here — 
they become native blocking links, so any ticket whose blockers are done is on the frontier and multiple 
agents can work in parallel. Prefer many thin AFK slices over a few thick HITL ones — but do prefer fat 
vertical slices over thin horizontal ones, which delay feedback on real user value. Everything /to-tickets 
creates is an implementation ticket — the counterpart of the wayfinder’s decision tickets, and a 
distinction worth keeping sharp: a decision ticket can only close by making its decision; an 
implementation ticket closes when that decision is reified in code. 
Two refinements. First, look for chances to prefactor — “make the change easy, then make the easy 
change.” Second, a wide refactor — one mechanical change (rename a column, retype a shared 
symbol) whose blast radius fans across the whole codebase — is the one exception to vertical slicing: 
no slice can land green. Sequence it as expand–contract: expand (add the new form beside the old so 
nothing breaks), migrate call sites in batches sized by blast radius — each batch its own issue, CI 
staying green because the old form survives — then contract (delete the old form once no caller

Phase 6 — Implement: HITL first, then AFK 
Goal: build the slices with a tight feedback loop — watched at first, autonomous once trusted. Kick each 
issue off with /implement, passing it the spec and the single ticket: it drives /tdd internally at the pre-
agreed seams — one red-green slice at a time — runs typechecks and single test files as it goes and 
the full suite once at the end, then closes out with /code-review before committing; for hard bugs reach 
for /diagnosing-bugs. 
The red / green loop (refactor moved to review) 
• Red: write a failing test first and run it to confirm it fails. This proves the test is wired to real 
behavior — and, for a bug, proves the bug exists. 
• Green: write the minimum implementation to pass. The failing test gives the agent a concrete 
target it can run, inspect, log against, and use as feedback. 
• Refactor: now deliberately out of the implementation loop — it happens at code review, where 
the smells are named and fixed in a fresh session, so implementation stays focused. When you 
do refactor, the old rule holds: keep the suite green, never refactor while red. 
Combine red/green with tracer bullets: one test for one vertical slice, make it pass, then the next — 
never a wide horizontal layer of shallow tests. Test behavior through public interfaces, not 
implementation details, so tests survive refactors and read like specifications. Tests live at seams — 
the public boundaries agreed in the spec — never against internals; no test gets written at an 
unconfirmed seam. Watch for the rhythm (fail before implementation, pass after); it’s hard to fake when 
the test is meaningful. 
Pre-commit hooks — a deterministic safety net 
/setup-pre-commit wires Husky to run typecheck, tests, and lint-staged (Prettier) on every commit; if 
any step fails the commit is blocked and the agent gets the error back to act on. 
The counterintuitive insight. Pre-commit hooks are famously annoying for humans — 
three minutes of waiting per commit — but perfect for agents. “Claude doesn’t care how long 
it takes. It doesn’t get frustrated. The friction that’s painful for a human developer is actually 
perfect for an agent.” 
Browser tools — visual feedback loops 
When the agent can see its own rendered output, the feedback loop tightens further. This is the visual 
equivalent of a failing test: a concrete target the agent can run, look at, and correct. Two routes exist, 
and it pays to know which is which. In VS Code, Copilot’s built-in browser tools (generally available 
since July 1, 2026) let the agent open the page, inspect what it built, and iterate against the real DOM 
— point it at the running app and describe what’s wrong (“the months aren’t evenly spaced; there’s 
room for another row of years”). Burke flags the honest limitation: clicking an element in that browser 
feeds the VS Code sidebar chat, not the CLI — a hole in the CLI workflow he works around by

Going AFK — autopilot and the Ralph loop 
“Do phase N” is really a for-loop waiting to be automated. Around December 2025 the models became 
good enough to send off on well-defined tasks, and Geoffrey Huntley’s “Ralph Wiggum” technique — a 
simple loop running the same prompt over and over to grind through a tranche of work — is the pattern. 
The key detail: each iteration gets a fresh context window (a bash loop, not one long-lived session held 
open by a stop hook), so every run stays in the efficient zone. AFK agents let you delegate huge 
amounts of work while you plan or QA other runs in parallel. 
In Copilot CLI the loop is native. Switch to autopilot (Shift+Tab cycles standard → plan → autopilot) and 
it works through each step until it judges the goal complete, or drive it headless: copilot -p "<prompt>" 
--autopilot --allow-all-tools --max-autopilot-continues 10. Programmatic runs must grant tool 
access explicitly (--allow-all-tools, or the COPILOT_ALLOW_ALL environment variable); --max-autopilot-
continues N caps the iterations — the default is unlimited, so always cap unattended runs; --no-ask-
user stops the agent pausing on questions; -s/--silent emits only the final message for scripting; and -
-share/--share-gist writes the session log somewhere a human can review it later. /fleet fans 
independent slices across parallel subagents, and for a Ralph loop proper you wrap the headless 
command in a bash loop so each iteration starts from clean context. (Experimental prompt scheduling 
— /after and /every — can queue or cron follow-up prompts harness-side; experimental hooks and 
plugins extend the harness the way Part II’s rings predict.) 
Pulling from a backlog 
An agent you must point at a plan each time isn’t autonomous. Instead it pulls tasks from a queue — 
your issues — and decides what to do next, prioritizing in this order: (1) critical bug fixes, (2) 
development infrastructure (tests, types, dev scripts), (3) tracer bullets (new features), (4) polish and 
quick wins, (5) refactors. After each task it commits with a detailed message (key decisions, files 
changed, notes for the next run), closes or comments the issue, and moves on — one task per run. 
Filter available work with a ready-for-agent label. The pattern holds up to roughly 20–30 open tasks. 
Why it works. This is a virtuous loop: the agent produces code, a human reviews it, new 
issues get created, and the agent picks up the next task — productivity that compounds 
while you’re elsewhere. One real project (the Sandcastle runner) was built this way across 
403 closed issues. 
Sandboxing 
An AFK agent running without permission prompts has a lot of freedom — it could delete directories or, 
if prompt-injected, exfiltrate code. A sandbox confines it to the files you share; it can still explore, run 
bash, and use git, but can’t harm the rest of the system. OS-level sandboxes (Docker, Podman, E2B) 
are much harder to escape than rules alone — agents can break out of software-only sandboxes if they 
try. A sandbox-agnostic runner lets you start with no sandbox to focus on the prompt, then add isolation 
without changing agent code. 
In Copilot CLI you keep this safe by suppressing permission prompts only inside the box: /yolo (or --
yolo, now with on|off|show) is shorthand for --allow-all, which expands to --allow-all-tools --allow-
all-paths --allow-all-urls. Run the agent in a dev container so the worst case is a destroyed 
container, and add --deny-tool rules as a deterministic guardrail — deny always beats allow, and the 
Kind(argument) syntax scopes precisely, e.g. --deny-tool='shell(git push)'.

Handoff — expand, then contract 
Mid-session you’ll spot a side task — a broken test, a file to update — that deserves its own context 
window. Clearing would lose the main task; doing it inline may exhaust the budget. /handoff writes a 
temporary markdown doc (in the OS temp dir, not your repo) so a fresh agent can continue: it suggests 
which skills the next session should use, references existing artifacts instead of duplicating them, and 
redacts secrets. This enables “expand then contract” flows — hand off from a long grill into a focused 
sub-session (grill one part, prototype, burn context), then hand a condensed doc back. Reach for it 
whenever a session drifts into the inefficient zone. 
Phase 7 — Review and continuous improvement 
Goal: check the work against both the spec and your standards, then feed lessons back in. In this guide 
the review phase is layered: /code-review is the mandatory gate on every ticket; /rubber-duck is 
Copilot’s cross-model escalation for high-stakes or design-heavy changes; and Burke’s multi-model 
refinement loop is the hardening pass you run on a finished feature. 
Layer 1 — /code-review, the gate on every ticket. Review along two axes: Standards (does the code 
follow this repo’s documented conventions?) and Spec (does it faithfully implement the originating spec 
or ticket?). /code-review does exactly this: it pins the diff against a fixed point (a branch, commit, tag, or 
merge-base), finds the originating spec and the repo’s standards sources, and runs both axes as 
parallel sub-agents so neither pollutes the other. On top of whatever the repo documents, the 
Standards axis carries a fixed baseline of Fowler code smells (Refactoring, ch. 3) — always judgement 
calls, never hard violations, and a documented repo standard overrides the baseline. The two reports 
are never merged or reranked: a change can pass one axis and fail the other, and reporting them 
separately stops one from masking the other. This is also where refactoring now lives: the 
implementation does the red-green; the automated review does the refactor, naming the smells and 
fixing them in a fresh session. And this phase is where the harness earns its keep — with code review, 
the harness matters more than the model: the pinned diff, the located sources, and the two parallel 
axes do more for review quality than a smarter model would. The cheapest bug-catcher of all is the 
second pass itself — reading the finished work against the spec catches what the implementation 
session cannot see. (Copilot CLI has also grown an experimental native /review command — a 
harness-side complement, not a replacement for the two-axis skill.) 
Keep the vocabulary straight: an automated check is deterministic (tests, types, lint, build, pre-commit 
— pass/fail); an automated review is an agent judging another agent’s work (non-deterministic); a 
human review means reading the diff — reading the summary doesn’t count; and accepting code with 
no review at all is vibe coding. 
Where do coding standards live? Push them into the reviewer, not the implementer. The implementing 
agent’s context is already strained — every standard loaded there competes with the actual task — 
while the reviewer reads them fresh. Run review in a cleared session so it happens in the efficient 
zone. Mine the standards from your own PR review comments — the things you actually flag — never 
by asking an agent to generate them from the codebase. And hold one line: humans should only touch 
AI-reviewed code. 
Layer 2 — /rubber-duck, the cross-model escalation. Copilot CLI is multi-model, and /rubber-duck 
exploits that: it hands the current plan, implementation, or tests to a different model family than the one 
driving your session, so the critic doesn’t share the orchestrator’s blind spots — in Burke’s demo, a 
Claude-family (Opus) planning session critiqued by a GPT-family model (“GPT-5.5”). Different families 
have different training data and different holes; the hybrid review costs a little more and is far stronger. 
Reach for it when the change is high-stakes or design-heavy — an architecture decision, a security-
sensitive diff, a plan you’re about to hand to autopilot — on top of, not instead of, the /code-review gate.

Layer 3 — the multi-model refinement loop. To harden a finished feature, run Burke’s review loop on 
autopilot: ask the agent to review the project for completeness, correctness, security, and 
improvements; get a contrasting model family’s input on the findings; list them by priority; fix them all; 
and repeat until both models agree the only items left have diminishing return. That phrase — 
“diminishing return” — is the stop condition: models always find something when asked to review, so 
without it the loop never ends. In the demo this ran three rounds and surfaced keyboard accessibility, 
two-digit year handling, several bugs, year-bound clamping, and HTML escaping — issues that would 
otherwise have shown up in production. It burns two top-tier models, so spend it on features that 
warrant it.

session history and suggests how to work better — its subcommands are standup | tips | improve | 
search | reindex: /chronicle tips surfaces patterns like a prompt you’ve sent a dozen times that belongs 
in an instruction file, /chronicle improve flags where the agent misread your intent, /chronicle standup 
summarizes recent work, and /chronicle search (added in v1.0.49) queries the indexed history directly. 
Bake the suggestions back into your AGENTS.md and project skills, and feed new domain terms, 
rejected designs, and architectural friction into the next Grill — that compounding loop is the whole

Deep modules — designing for AX 
A deep module (Ousterhout) is a thin interface over a large hidden implementation. Hide as much as 
possible behind a simple, rarely-changing interface, and the payoff is large: callers depend only on the 
interface; you can test huge units at the boundary; tests survive internal refactors; and the agent reads 
one interface to know what the module does and where to change. The skill’s precise vocabulary is 
worth adopting — Module (anything with an interface and an implementation), Interface (everything a 
caller must know), Seam (where behavior can change without editing in place — Michael Feathers’ 
term), Adapter, Depth (how much leverage the interface gives), and Locality — because “vague 
architecture words create vague refactors.” 
/improve-codebase-architecture scopes its scan to where change is landing — YAGNI, not a whole-
repo audit — and surfaces clusters of shallow modules, spawns parallel sub-agents to design 
competing interfaces (this is Ousterhout’s “design it twice”), presents the candidates as a visual HTML 
report, then grills you on the one you pick and records the decision as an ADR / glossary update. Run it 
every couple of days in a fast-moving repo (weekly at minimum), after a surge of development — and 
consider a pass during planning, where a deepening opportunity can reshape the tickets. Treat your 
deep modules as grey boxes: you can look inside, but you trust the implementation by default and 
review the tests more than the internals — you own the interface, the agent owns the implementation, 
and the tests keep it honest. 
The principle. Spotting that a cluster of modules should become one deep module still 
requires human architectural insight — the agent can’t do it unaided yet. You become the 
architect and interface designer; the agent becomes the implementation specialist. And the 
continuous-improvement loop is the whole point: feed new domain terms, rejected designs, 
and architectural friction back into the next Grill so the system compounds and gets better.

The Wayfinder Map — when the idea is bigger than a session 
Sometimes the loose idea is bigger than any one session can hold — wrapped in fog, with the way from 
here to the destination not yet visible. Wayfinding is about finding the way, not charging at the 
destination. /wayfinder charts that way as a shared map on the issue tracker, then works it one ticket 
per session until the way is clear. It is the second front door of the main flow: where /grill-with-docs 
sharpens an idea you can hold in one session, wayfinder is for the idea you can’t — and in practice it 
has become Matt’s default entry for anything non-trivial, since it degrades gracefully: if it senses no 
map is needed, it simply runs as a plain grilling session. Where it differs from a plain grill is scale — a 
greenfield project, a huge feature build — and grilling, research, and prototyping become tickets on a 
map instead of one-off sessions. The destination is a locked set of decisions plus a spec you can hand 
to an implementation agent — execution is out of scope: the map produces decisions, not deliverables. 
When the map is done it hands off, it doesn’t build: merge onto the main flow at /to-spec (or straight to 
/implement if the effort turned out small). Instead of managing session anxiety — handoffs, the zone 
budget — everything is saved on the tracker: close a session, open the next ticket. The map is done 
when nothing is left to decide before someone goes and does the thing; the pull to just do the work is 
usually the signal you have reached the map’s edge and it is time to hand off into a spec and Phases 
4–6, the closed tickets riding along as primary sources. The handoff is mechanical: point /to-spec at the 
map in a fresh session, let it read the map and the closed tickets, and write the spec — then close the 
map; its job is done, and a lingering map is doc rot. 
 
The wayfinder map: typed decision tickets walked one session at a time until the way to the spec is 
clear.

The map is a single issue on the repo’s issue tracker, labelled wayfinder:map — the canonical artifact. 
In this guide’s GitHub-native shape, the map is a parent GitHub Issue and every decision ticket is a 
sub-issue of it, carrying a type label wayfinder:<type>. The map is an index, not a store. It names the 
destination — settled first, because it fixes the scope — keeps a one-line gist plus link for every 
decision made, and carries two honest lists: a “## Not yet specified” section (the in-scope fog) and a 
“## Out of scope” section (work consciously ruled beyond the destination, which never graduates back 
in). Each decision ticket holds one question — a question whose resolution is a decision, not a slice of 
a build to execute — sized to one agent session, and typed: 
• Research (wayfinder:research, AFK) — resolved by a /research subagent working primary 
sources, each committing a cited findings file on a branch the ticket links to. In Copilot, run these 
as custom agents or fan them across /fleet — or lean on the CLI’s experimental native /research. 
• Prototype (wayfinder:prototype, HITL) — raise the fidelity of the discussion with a cheap artifact 
to react to. 
• Grilling (wayfinder:grilling, HITL) — the default type. 
• Task (wayfinder:task, HITL or AFK) — the one type that does rather than decides: manual work 
that unblocks a decision. 
HITL means the ticket only resolves through that live exchange; the agent never stands in for the 
human’s side — a grilling agent that answers its own questions has broken the contract. 
The map is deliberately incomplete: don’t plan past the fog of war. Beyond the live tickets lies fog — 
decisions you can tell are coming but can’t yet pin down. Fog or ticket? The test is whether you can 
state the question precisely now — not whether you can answer it now. The frontier is the open, 
unblocked, unclaimed child tickets — the edge of the known. Resolving one clears the fog ahead of it, 
graduating newly specifiable questions into fresh tickets; blocking uses the tracker’s native dependency 
relationship — GitHub’s issue dependencies between sub-issues — so the frontier renders visually in 
GitHub’s own UI. “Resolve just the decisions at the frontier first.” 
The discipline: charting the map is one session’s work (grill the destination, grill the frontier breadth-
first, create the tickets, wire the blocking edges — and stop); charting fires all /research subagents in 
parallel the moment the map is charted, but hand-resolves nothing. Thereafter never resolve more than 
one ticket per session — with the exception of research tickets, whose subagents run in parallel — and 
a session claims a ticket by assigning it to itself FIRST, before working it, so parallel sessions skip it. 
Each resolution posts the answer as a comment, closes the ticket, and appends a one-line pointer to 
the map. A ticket can also schedule new tickets mid-flight: when working one decision surfaces a 
question this session can’t hold, write the findings into the current ticket, close it, and open the new 
question onto the map — the thing a plain grilling session never had anywhere to put. In everything a 
human reads, refer to tickets by name, never bare ids — a wall of #42, #43, #44 is illegible. If the 
frontier surfaces no fog at all, the journey fits one session and you don’t need a map. 
Why it works. The map is progressive disclosure applied to planning: each session loads 
the low-res map, zooms into exactly one ticket, and leaves one decision behind — so a 
hundred grilling, prototyping, and research sessions can contribute to one central map that 
grows as fog clears and shrinks as questions close. (Matt’s first real use planned an entire 
course this way.) And because the map lives on GitHub Issues, the frontier is visible to 
collaborators in the tracker’s own UI — expect other sessions to be editing it concurrently.

Phase 0 — setup (once per machine, once per repo) 
• Install the skills: npx skills@latest add mattpocock/skills (the Vercel Labs skills.sh installer —

• Stand up the supporting system: run /setup-matt-pocock-skills once inside each repo. It walks

• Add the deterministic safety net: run /setup-pre-commit, and set standing --deny-tool rules 
(deny always beats allow) so formatting, type errors, and dangerous git commands never reach a

Greenfield vs brownfield 
On an existing (brownfield) codebase, start with /grill-with-docs to build the glossary as you plan your 
first change. On a new (greenfield) project, build the supporting docs at the earliest possible moment — 
grill, save findings to research files, prototype the risky ideas, define the module shape, then write the 
spec — so everything downstream already speaks the right domain language. Establishing 
CONTEXT.md and ADRs during the initial grill (not after the spec) is what keeps later tickets from using 
stale terminology. Alternatively, use /wayfinder to plot the course for your whole app. Don’t over-index 
on the distinction, though — “brownfield versus greenfield is kind of BS”: the workflow is identical, the 
fundamentals transfer, and wayfinder runs as happily on a decade-old codebase as on an empty folder. 
What changes is only where the first domain terms come from. 
From HITL to AFK — earn the autonomy 
Begin human-in-the-loop: watch the agent work a slice, build trust, and feel where your taste is required 
(planning, QA, architecture, UX). As trust grows, move well-defined AFK slices into autopilot or the 
Ralph loop in a sandbox, and parallelize — plan the next feature or QA another run while the agent 
ships. HITL and AFK are first-class citizens of the same workflow; orchestrate a hybrid rather than 
choosing one. 
The grill / execute / clear rhythm 
For a small feature or bug fix that fits one window: grill to alignment, implement, QA in the same 
window, then clear and move on. For anything larger: grill → spec → tickets in one unbroken window, 
then run each issue in a fresh context. Clear early — around 34–40% of the window in HITL 
implementation — accepting the small cost of a re-explore in exchange for staying firmly in the efficient 
zone. 
Working as a team 
The workflow is collaborative up to the spec and single-owner after it. Grill as a group — mob grilling 
with a domain expert in the room (or a meeting transcript pasted into the session) is the highest-
bandwidth way to load the design concept — then let one engineer own the implementation, because a 
shared HITL session has no pilot. Standards are a team asset: keep one company-wide set, mined

Part VII · Sandcastle — The AFK Software Factory 
Part IV ended Phase 6 with a promise: once you trust the loop, you go AFK — away from keyboard — 
and let agents pull work from a queue. Sandcastle is the machine that makes that safe and repeatable. 
It is Matt’s open-source answer to the question “how do I run coding agents unattended without 
handing them the keys to my laptop?” This chapter is a standalone guide to it. 
Why Sandcastle exists 
Running an agent unattended has two failure modes. The first is danger: an agent in “yolo” mode (all 
permissions granted, no human in the loop) can delete your home directory, leak secrets, or run 
destructive commands. The second is friction: the existing ways to sandbox an agent — raw Docker, 
vendor cloud tools — took a lot of configuration and were hard to own. Matt wanted something that was 
simple, scriptable, agent-agnostic, and entirely yours: just code you can read and change. That is 
Sandcastle. 
The one-line description from the project: a TypeScript library for orchestrating AI coding agents in 
isolated sandboxes. You invoke an agent with a single call; Sandcastle handles isolating it with a 
configurable branch strategy; the commits it makes get merged back. It is good for parallelizing multiple 
AFK agents, building review pipelines, or just orchestrating your own agents. 
The mental model and vocabulary 
Sandcastle ships a deliberate, precise vocabulary (it practices the ubiquitous-language discipline the 
rest of this guide preaches). The terms worth knowing before you read any example: 
• Sandcastle — the tool that orchestrates an agent inside a sandbox. 
• Agent — the AI coding tool invoked inside the sandbox (GitHub Copilot CLI, Claude Code, 
Codex, and others). It is swappable; nothing below is harness-specific. 
• Sandbox — the isolation boundary around the agent: a container, VM, or similar environment that 
constrains what it can touch. 
• Host — your machine, where Sandcastle runs and the real git repo lives. 
• Sandbox provider — a pluggable implementation that creates the sandbox, passed to run() via 
the sandbox option. Three shapes: a bind-mount provider mounts the host filesystem into the 
environment (Docker, Podman); an isolated provider has its own filesystem and syncs code in 
and commits out (Vercel); a no-sandbox provider runs the agent directly on the host with no 
container. 
• Branch strategy — how the agent’s changes relate to git branches. head works directly in the 
host working directory; merge-to-head creates a temporary branch, lets the agent work on it, then 
merges back to your current branch; branch lands commits on an explicitly named branch you 
provide. 
• Worktree — a git worktree under .sandcastle/worktrees/ on the host, used by the merge-to-head 
and branch strategies so the agent never works directly on your checked-out branch. 
• Iteration — a single invocation of the agent inside the sandbox, producing at most one commit 
against one task (a work item the agent selects, typically from an issue tracker). 
• Completion signal — a marker the agent emits (default <promise>COMPLETE</promise>) to end the 
iteration loop early. Structured output is the orthogonal idea: a schema-validated JSON payload 
the agent emits inside a named XML tag, returned to the caller.

Quick start 
Sandcastle is an npm package. Install it, scaffold a config directory, add your token, and run a 
TypeScript file: 
npm install --save-dev @ai-hero/sandcastle 
npx @ai-hero/sandcastle init        # scaffolds a .sandcastle/ directory 
# edit .sandcastle/.env: set a GitHub token (COPILOT_GITHUB_TOKEN / GH_TOKEN) 
#   (a fine-grained PAT with the Copilot Requests permission, or any token from `gh auth login`) 
npx tsx .sandcastle/main.ts          # run the agent 
The init command interactively asks three questions: which agent (GitHub Copilot CLI, Claude Code, 
Codex), which sandbox provider (Docker is the first-class local option), and which issue tracker / 
backlog (GitHub issues is the default; issues are filtered by a sandcastle label). It writes a .sandcastle/ 
folder containing a Dockerfile, an .env, and a main.ts you run with tsx. 
The smallest meaningful program is one run() call: 
import { run, copilot } from "@ai-hero/sandcastle"; 
import { docker } from "@ai-hero/sandcastle/sandboxes/docker"; 
await run({ 
  agent: copilot("claude-opus-4-8"), 
  sandbox: docker(),            // or podman(), vercel(), noSandbox() 
  promptFile: ".sandcastle/prompt.md", 
}); 
The run() API, in brief 
run() takes an agent and a sandbox (both required) plus a prompt (inline prompt or promptFile). The 
options you reach for most: 
• maxIterations — how many times the agent runs before stopping (default 1). Each iteration 
works one task and makes at most one commit. 
• branchStrategy — head, merge-to-head, or an explicit branch (defaults: head for bind-mount 
providers, merge-to-head for isolated ones). 
• hooks — lifecycle commands grouped by where they run: host hooks (e.g. copy an .env into the 
worktree) and sandbox hooks (e.g. npm install on onSandboxReady). 
• completionSignal — the string(s) the agent emits to end the loop early. idleTimeoutSeconds fails 
a run that has gone silent; completionTimeoutSeconds is the grace window after the completion 
signal is seen but the process has not yet exited (a “hanging process” — usually a spawned gh/git 
child holding stdout open). 
• output — Output.object({ tag, schema }) or Output.string({ tag }) to extract typed, schema-
validated structured output from the agent’s stdout (requires maxIterations === 1, and the tag 
must appear in the prompt). 
run() returns the iterations executed (with per-iteration usage), the commits created, and the target 
branch — so a harness can inspect exactly what happened. 
Sandbox providers 
The sandbox option accepts any provider; the built-ins cover most needs: 
• Docker — bind-mount; the most common local choice, shown in nearly every example. 
• Podman — bind-mount; a rootless alternative to Docker. 
• Vercel — isolated; cloud Firecracker microVMs via @vercel/sandbox.

The pipeline it unlocks: plan → implement → review → merge 
A single run() is the primitive. The pattern demonstrates is a multi-agent factory built from it, mirroring 
the seven-phase workflow: 
• Planner. One agent reads the open, labeled issues and emits a plan (wrapped in a tag, as 
structured output) of the unblocked issues to work — a DAG, not a flat list. 
• Implementers, in parallel. Each issue spawns its own agent on its own branch, prompted with 
the issue title and id, working red-green via /tdd. 
• Reviewer. A reviewer agent runs against each branch’s diff, checking it against the spec and the 
repo’s coding standards — the two-axis /code-review discipline — fixing problems, and reporting 
to the human. (Use a different, sometimes more expensive, model here than for implementation.) 
• Merger. A final agent pulls the branches back to the main branch, resolving merge conflicts via 
/resolving-merge-conflicts and closing issues with comments. 
Because it is “just code,” you can rearrange these freely: adversarial cross-agent review (implement 
with one model, review with another), or several agents proposing competing implementations while a 
reviewer picks the best. 
For the common implement-then-review sequence, createSandbox() keeps a single container warm 
across multiple run() calls, so dependencies and build artifacts persist instead of paying container boot 
each phase. sandbox.exec() runs a shell command in that same warm sandbox — handy to gate the 
review on a quick test pass: 
await using sandbox = await createSandbox({ branch: "agent/fix-42", sandbox: docker() }); 
await sandbox.run({ agent: copilot("claude-opus-4-8"), promptFile: ".sandcastle/implement.md", 
maxIterations: 5 }); 
const tests = await sandbox.exec("npm test");        // non-zero exitCode is returned, not thrown 
if (tests.exitCode !== 0) throw new Error(tests.stdout); 
await sandbox.run({ agent: copilot("gpt-5.5"), prompt: "Review the changes and fix any issues." });

Sandcastle is the engine behind Phase 6 (Implement: HITL first, then AFK) and Phase 7 (Review). The

Sidebar: running Sandcastle with GitHub Copilot CLI 
Copilot CLI is a first-class, shipping agent provider in Sandcastle — copilot() sits beside claudeCode() 
and codex(), and for each iteration it runs copilot -p with --allow-all-tools and your chosen --model 
inside the sandbox. A few Copilot-specific details are worth knowing: 
• Install Copilot CLI inside the image, not just on the host. The init command already does this 
— its Dockerfile runs RUN npm install -g @github/copilot — but if you hand-roll the image, add 
that line or you get sh: copilot: command not found. 
• Pass a GitHub token into the container. Drop a token with the Copilot Requests permission into 
.sandcastle/.env; Sandcastle reads it in precedence COPILOT_GITHUB_TOKEN > GH_TOKEN > 
GITHUB_TOKEN, and the bundled gh uses the same token to read your GitHub Issues backlog. 
• Expect no session resume. Copilot CLI indexes session state in a SQLite DB, so Sandcastle 
marks the copilot() provider non-resumable: multi-iteration runs still work, but each iteration starts 
fresh with no conversation carryover (often what you want anyway) and there is no captured per-
iteration token usage. 
• Keep prompts under ~120 KB. The prompt is passed via the -p argv argument, not stdin, so 
very large prompts throw a clear guard error — keep them lean and push detail into research.md 
and the issue bodies the agent reads itself. (And don’t copy host node_modules into a Linux 
container; let it run npm install.) 
Gotchas worth knowing 
• Worktree locking. git refuses to check out the same branch in two worktrees at once; if a branch 
is already checked out in your main working tree, pick a different branch or switch first. 
• Hanging processes vs stuck agents. A finished agent whose child process keeps stdout open 
is resolved by completionTimeoutSeconds (a success, with a warning), distinct from a genuinely 
silent agent that hits the idle timeout (a failure). 
• Resume and structured-output retries (v0.11). sandbox.run supports resuming a session inside 
a warm container, and Output.object/Output.string gained maxRetries that re-feeds extraction 
errors to the agent — both require a resume-capable agent provider (claudeCode, codex); the 
copilot() provider is non-resumable, so it skips these.

Reach for Sandcastle when you have a real backlog of well-specified, independent issues and you trust 
your feedback loops (types, tests, review) enough to let agents run unattended. Stay human-in-the-loop 
— and skip the AFK machinery — while you are still grilling, prototyping, or working on anything whose

before expanding (Pragmatic Programmer). 
Deep module A thin interface over a large hidden implementation (Ousterhout). The 
core of an AI-friendly codebase. 
Seam A place where behavior can change without editing in place (Michael 
Feathers). 
Grey box A deep module you can look inside but trust by default — you review 
its tests more than its internals. 
HITL / AFK Human-in-the-loop (you pair with the agent) vs away-from-keyboard 
(you kick it off and leave). In a wayfinder ticket, HITL means it only 
resolves through that live exchange — the agent never stands in for 
the human’s side. 
Ralph loop Geoffrey Huntley’s pattern: a bash loop running the same prompt 
repeatedly, each iteration in a fresh context window. 
AX Agent experience — how well the environment (checks, architecture, 
free context) lets an agent do good work. 
Automated check / 
review / human 
review / vibe coding 
Deterministic pass-fail; an agent judging an agent; a human reading 
the diff; and accepting code with no review at all. 
Wayfinder map / 
destination 
What a wayfinder effort is finding its way to (a locked set of decisions 
plus a spec — execution out of scope), and the tracker issue that 
indexes the route: decisions so far, live tickets, fog, out-of-scope. On 
GitHub: a parent issue labelled wayfinder:map with sub-issue decision 
tickets. 
Frontier / fog of war The open, unblocked, unclaimed tickets at the edge of the known; and 
the in-scope decisions you can’t yet phrase sharply (the map’s “## Not 
yet specified” section). “Don’t plan past the fog of war.” 
Decision ticket / 
implementation ticket 
A wayfinder ticket closes only by making its decision; an 
implementation ticket (from /to-tickets) closes when the decision is 
reified in code. Decision tickets chart the way; implementation tickets 
walk it. 
Capture, don’t 
dispose 
Prototype doctrine: fold the validated decision into real code, commit 
the prototype to a throwaway branch off main as a primary source, 
and leave a context pointer on the implementation issue — main 
keeps only the decision.

Appendix B - The Reading List 
The workflow is, in effect, an applied reading list. These are the texts it draws on: 
• The Pragmatic Programmer — David Thomas & Andrew Hunt. Tracer bullets, “no-one knows 
exactly what they want,” small deliberate steps, software entropy. 
• The Design of Design — Frederick P. Brooks. The shared design concept and the design tree — 
grilling’s decision tree — behind it. 
• Domain-Driven Design — Eric Evans. Ubiquitous language, bounded contexts, context maps. 
• A Philosophy of Software Design — John Ousterhout. Deep modules and “design it twice.” 
• Extreme Programming Explained — Kent Beck. “Invest in the design of the system every day” 
and the TDD lineage. 
• Working Effectively with Legacy Code — Michael Feathers. Seams. 
• Refactoring — Martin Fowler. Keeping changes small and the suite green; chapter 3’s code 
smells are /code-review’s Standards baseline. 
• Shape Up — Ryan Singer. The fidelity ladder (text → wireframe → prototype → real app) behind 
prototyping at the right resolution.
